Is AI Telling Prospects the Wrong Price? How to Check and Fix It

Metricus · March 2026 · 4 min read

In this article

  1. The pricing misinformation problem
  2. Most common AI errors
  3. Where errors come from
  4. What wrong pricing costs you
  5. How to fix it

The pricing misinformation problem

When Metricus ran AI visibility reports across B2B SaaS companies, we found that the majority had at least one pricing error in AI-generated responses. In one case, AI told prospects $30/user when the actual price was $10. In another, it described a free tier that had been discontinued two years earlier. These are not minor discrepancies — they are factual errors that directly affect buyer decisions.

What we found: Across our audit sample, AI pricing errors fell into predictable patterns — outdated pricing from cached review sites, conflated tiers, and competitive misinformation from comparison articles.

The most common AI pricing errors

Our audits identified five recurring error types. The frequency of pricing errors surprised even brands with sophisticated marketing operations — most had never checked what AI says about their pricing, and many were unaware of the degree to which stale third-party sources were shaping buyer perceptions.

  • Outdated pricing: AI cites prices from 12–24 months ago, typically sourced from cached G2 or Capterra listings that have not been updated.
  • Tier conflation: AI merges features from different pricing tiers into a single description, telling buyers a basic plan includes enterprise features or vice versa.
  • Discontinued plans: AI recommends free tiers or plans that no longer exist, sourced from old blog posts or comparison articles.
  • Competitor-sourced misinformation: AI pulls pricing details from competitor comparison pages that overstate your price to make their own offering look better.
  • Currency confusion: AI reports prices in the wrong currency or without specifying currency, creating confusion for international buyers.

Where do these errors come from?

What we found is that AI models pull pricing information from whatever sources they can find during retrieval. The most common sources: G2 and Capterra listing pages (often 6–18 months stale), competitor comparison blog posts, old press releases announcing pricing changes, and cached versions of your own pricing page. The AI has no way to know which source is current. It treats the most frequently cited number as the “consensus” price, even when that number is wrong.

What wrong pricing actually costs

When AI tells a prospect your product costs 3x what it actually costs, that prospect is either eliminated from your pipeline or arrives with incorrect expectations. When AI tells a prospect your product is cheaper than it is, you get a lead who discovers the real price during sales and feels misled. Neither outcome is good. What we found in our data is that pricing accuracy is one of the highest-impact elements of AI visibility because it directly affects conversion at the point of purchase consideration.

How pricing errors propagate

What we found is that pricing errors in AI follow a specific propagation pattern. A single stale source — often a G2 listing or an old comparison blog post — gets picked up by multiple AI models during their training data collection. Once embedded in training data, the wrong price persists across model versions until the training pipeline ingests enough contradictory information to override it. Meanwhile, RAG-driven responses may cite the same stale source in real time, reinforcing the error.

The propagation effect means that a pricing error is not just wrong once — it is wrong repeatedly, across platforms, for months. A buyer asking ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini about your pricing may receive the same wrong number from all three, creating the impression of consensus when there is none. What we found in our data is that pricing errors discovered in one AI platform existed in at least two others 60% of the time.

Industries most affected

Pricing misinformation affects some categories more than others. What we found is that B2B SaaS companies with usage-based pricing are the most frequently misrepresented, because AI models struggle to summarize variable pricing structures. Companies that have changed their pricing model within the past 18 months are also disproportionately affected, as older pricing information still dominates the indexed web. E-commerce brands with frequent promotions see AI cite regular prices, promotional prices, and competitor prices interchangeably.

Understanding where AI gets your pricing wrong — and which sources are feeding the errors — is foundational to correcting the problem.
